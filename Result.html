Landslide Susceptibility

Landslides are sudden and rapid movements of soil, rock, or debris down a slope, which are considered in landslide susceptibility mapping to identify areas prone to such events.

Landslides

Landslides impact landslide susceptibility mapping in the following ways:

  • Historical landslide data: The occurrence and distribution of past landslides provide valuable information for assessing future landslide susceptibility. Analyzing historical landslide data helps identify areas with a higher likelihood of landslides and contributes to the development of susceptibility maps.
  • Identification of landslide-prone areas: Landslides serve as indicators of areas that are susceptible to slope instability. By mapping the locations and characteristics of landslides, susceptible areas can be identified and incorporated into landslide susceptibility assessments.
  • Triggering events analysis: Landslides are often triggered by specific events such as heavy rainfall, earthquakes, or human activities. Understanding the relationship between triggering events and landslide occurrences is crucial in landslide susceptibility mapping to identify areas more prone to specific triggers and assess their potential impact.
  • Identification of landslide types: Different types of landslides (e.g., debris flows, rockfalls, earth slides) exhibit distinct characteristics and occur under specific conditions. Mapping the distribution of landslide types helps refine landslide susceptibility assessments by considering the specific factors associated with each type.
  • Validation and calibration: Historical landslides serve as important data points for validating and calibrating landslide susceptibility models. By comparing the predicted susceptibility with the locations and characteristics of past landslides, the accuracy and reliability of the susceptibility mapping can be assessed and improved.
  • Land-use planning and risk management: Incorporating landslide data into susceptibility mapping allows for informed land-use planning and risk management. Areas with a high frequency of landslides or high susceptibility can be designated as restricted or regulated zones, helping to mitigate the potential impact of landslides on human lives, infrastructure, and the environment.

Landslides, as both indicators and factors of slope instability, provide critical insights and data that contribute to the accuracy and effectiveness of landslide susceptibility mapping.

Final Result:

In producing a landslide map for Como, Italy, various layers were utilized, including slope, roads, rivers, profile, population, plan, NDVI, faults, land use and land cover, DTM (Digital Terrain Model), and aspect. These layers provided essential geospatial data that allowed for the assessment and mapping of landslide susceptibility in the region. By integrating these layers, the study aimed to identify areas at risk of landslides and support informed decision-making regarding land-use planning, infrastructure development, and mitigation measures in Como.

Case study

Studying landslide susceptibility in Como, Italy is necessary due to its mountainous terrain, historical landslide events, high population and infrastructure vulnerability, land-use planning considerations, and the need to enhance preparedness and response capabilities. By analyzing susceptibility factors, Como can identify high-risk areas, prioritize mitigation measures, and reduce the impact of landslides on lives, infrastructure, and sustainable development.

Aspect

Aspect refers to the compass direction that a slope faces and influences landslide susceptibility mapping by affecting moisture distribution, sun exposure, vegetation patterns, snow accumulation and melt, and erosion patterns.

Aspect

Aspect, which refers to the compass direction that a slope faces, can impact landslide susceptibility mapping in the following ways:

  • Moisture distribution: Aspect influences the distribution of moisture on a slope. North-facing slopes tend to receive less direct sunlight, leading to slower evaporation and potentially higher moisture content. High moisture levels can reduce the stability of slope materials and increase landslide susceptibility.
  • Sun exposure: South-facing slopes receive more direct sunlight, resulting in faster evaporation and potentially drier conditions. Drier slopes may have reduced cohesion and increased susceptibility to landslides in certain geological and environmental contexts.
  • Vegetation patterns: Aspect affects vegetation patterns on slopes due to variations in sunlight exposure. Different types of vegetation can impact slope stability, with certain species providing root reinforcement and stability. Variations in vegetation cover related to aspect can contribute to variations in landslide susceptibility.
  • Snow accumulation and melt: Aspect influences snow accumulation and melt patterns on slopes. North-facing slopes generally retain snow longer, resulting in increased water content during melting periods. This additional water can contribute to higher pore pressures and reduced stability, potentially increasing landslide susceptibility.
  • Erosion patterns: Aspect can influence erosion patterns on slopes due to variations in wind and water exposure. Erosion removes soil or rock from the slope, weakening its stability. Variations in erosion related to aspect can affect the overall susceptibility to landslides.

In landslide susceptibility mapping, aspect is considered alongside other factors such as slope steepness, soil properties, geology, and precipitation patterns to provide a comprehensive understanding of the terrain's vulnerability to landslides.

DTM

Digital Terrain Models (DTMs) have a significant impact on landslide susceptibility mapping by providing detailed information on slope gradients, elevation changes, and topographic features that influence the stability of slopes and can help identify areas at higher risk of landslides.

DTM

Digital Terrain Models (DTMs) play a crucial role in landslide susceptibility mapping due to their ability to provide detailed information about the topography and terrain characteristics of an area. Here are some specific ways in which DTMs impact landslide susceptibility mapping:

  • Slope calculation: DTMs allow for accurate calculation of slope angles and gradients across a landscape. Steeper slopes are generally more susceptible to landslides, and DTMs provide the necessary data to assess the degree of slope steepness, which is a key factor in landslide susceptibility analysis.
  • Aspect determination: DTMs help determine the aspect or compass direction that a slope faces. This information is important as different aspects receive varying amounts of sunlight, affecting factors such as moisture content and vegetation distribution, which in turn influence slope stability and landslide susceptibility.
  • Topographic features: DTMs provide detailed information about various topographic features such as ridges, valleys, and concave/convex terrain. These features impact water flow patterns, accumulation of sediments, and the concentration of stress on slopes, all of which are important considerations in landslide susceptibility mapping.
  • Drainage analysis: DTMs enable the identification and analysis of drainage patterns, including the location of rivers, streams, and watercourses. Proper drainage is essential for slope stability, and understanding the hydrological characteristics of an area through DTMs helps in assessing the risk of landslides.
  • Landslide scar identification: DTMs assist in identifying existing landslide scars or areas that have previously experienced landslides. By overlaying historical landslide data on the DTM, patterns can be analyzed, and areas with a higher likelihood of future landslides can be identified.
  • Geotechnical analysis: DTMs can be used to extract information about soil types, rock formations, and geological structures. These factors significantly influence the stability of slopes and contribute to landslide susceptibility. Integrating geotechnical data with DTMs enhances the accuracy of susceptibility mapping.
  • 3D visualization: DTMs facilitate the creation of 3D visualizations, which provide a comprehensive view of the terrain. This helps in visualizing and interpreting the relationships between different factors contributing to landslide susceptibility, aiding in better decision-making and risk assessment.

In summary, DTMs provide essential data for analyzing slope characteristics, drainage patterns, topographic features, and geotechnical information, all of which are crucial in assessing landslide susceptibility. The detailed and accurate representation of the terrain through DTMs enhances the effectiveness of landslide susceptibility mapping efforts.

DUSAF

Land use and land cover influence landslide susceptibility mapping by impacting slope stability through vegetation cover, soil erosion, urbanization, and water management practices.

DUSAF

Land use and land cover play a significant role in landslide susceptibility mapping by influencing the stability of slopes and the likelihood of landslides. Here are some specific ways in which land use and land cover impact landslide susceptibility mapping:

  • Vegetation cover: Dense vegetation, such as forests or grasslands, can provide root reinforcement and stabilize slopes, reducing the risk of landslides. Vegetation intercepts rainfall, reducing erosion and water infiltration into the soil, which can help maintain slope stability. Areas with extensive deforestation or sparse vegetation cover are more susceptible to landslides.
  • Land management practices: Human activities like agriculture, construction, and deforestation can alter the natural landscape, potentially increasing landslide susceptibility. Clearing vegetation, improper land grading, and uncontrolled soil excavation can weaken slope stability, leading to higher landslide risks.
  • Urbanization and infrastructure: Urban development can significantly modify the natural terrain, leading to changes in slope stability. Construction of buildings, roads, and other infrastructure can alter drainage patterns, increase surface runoff, and introduce artificial slopes that may be more susceptible to landslides.
  • Soil erosion: Land use practices that contribute to soil erosion, such as intensive agriculture, overgrazing, or improper land management, can increase landslide susceptibility. Erosion removes the protective topsoil layer, weakening slope materials and reducing their stability.
  • Water management: Improper water management practices, such as inadequate drainage systems or alteration of natural watercourses, can lead to increased water accumulation on slopes. Excess water can saturate the soil, reducing its strength and cohesion, and significantly increasing landslide susceptibility.
  • Land cover changes: Changes in land cover, such as deforestation, urban expansion, or wildfire-induced vegetation loss, can alter the balance of natural processes and increase the susceptibility of slopes to landslides. Vegetation removal or disturbance disrupts the equilibrium and stability of slopes.
  • Land-use planning and zoning: Incorporating landslide susceptibility mapping into land-use planning and zoning can help guide development away from high-risk areas. By considering the susceptibility of slopes to landslides, appropriate measures can be taken to mitigate risks and protect communities and infrastructure.

It is important to integrate land use and land cover data with other factors, such as topography, geology, and rainfall patterns, to create comprehensive landslide susceptibility maps. The combination of these factors provides a more accurate understanding of the areas at higher risk of landslides and enables informed decision-making regarding land use and disaster risk reduction measures.

Faults

Faults, geological fractures in the Earth's crust, can influence landslide susceptibility mapping by creating zones of weakness and potential failure along their boundaries.

Faults

Faults, which are geological fractures in the Earth's crust, can have a significant impact on landslide susceptibility mapping in the following ways:

  • Zone of weakness: Faults create zones of weakness along their boundaries, where the rock layers may be fractured or weakened. These weakened zones can increase the likelihood of slope instability and landslides, as the faults provide pathways for the movement of water and contribute to the reduction of slope cohesion.
  • Influence on slope stability: Faults can disrupt the continuity and stability of slopes. They can intersect or cut across slope materials, leading to differential movement and increased stress concentrations. This disruption can reduce the overall slope stability and increase the potential for landslides.
  • Groundwater flow: Faults often act as conduits for the movement of groundwater. They can alter the flow paths of water, leading to variations in pore water pressure distribution within the slope. These changes in water flow patterns can significantly impact slope stability and increase the susceptibility to landslides.
  • Triggering events: Faults can influence the occurrence of triggering events such as earthquakes. Earthquakes along faults can generate strong ground shaking that can induce landslides in adjacent slopes. The presence of faults, especially active faults, can be an indicator of increased seismic activity and higher landslide susceptibility in the surrounding areas.
  • Landscape evolution: Over geologic timescales, faults can shape the landscape by creating distinctive landforms such as scarps, ridges, or valleys. These landforms can have unique characteristics that influence the potential for landslides. For example, fault scarps can act as potential failure planes or barriers that can affect the movement and stability of slopes.
  • Data integration: Incorporating fault data into landslide susceptibility mapping allows for a comprehensive assessment of the terrain. Combining fault location, geometry, and activity information with other factors such as topography, soil properties, and land cover can provide a more accurate understanding of the areas prone to landslides.

Understanding the presence, characteristics, and activity of faults is crucial in landslide susceptibility mapping. It helps identify areas where slope stability may be compromised and aids in assessing the potential risks associated with landslides.

NDVI

Normalised Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) is a remote sensing technique used in landslide susceptibility mapping to assess vegetation health and density, providing insights into slope stability and erosion control.

NDVI

Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) has a significant impact on landslide susceptibility mapping due to its ability to provide insights into vegetation health and density, which play a crucial role in slope stability. Here are some specific ways in which NDVI impacts landslide susceptibility mapping:

  • Vegetation cover assessment: NDVI measures the density and health of vegetation by analyzing the difference in reflectance between near-infrared and red light. Vegetation acts as a natural stabilizer of slopes, and areas with dense and healthy vegetation cover are generally more resistant to landslides. NDVI helps identify areas with robust vegetation cover, which indicates a lower susceptibility to landslides.
  • Erosion control: Vegetation plays a crucial role in controlling erosion by minimizing the impact of rainfall on the soil surface. NDVI can identify areas with sparse or degraded vegetation, indicating a higher susceptibility to erosion and potential landslides. Mapping NDVI data helps prioritize areas for erosion control measures, such as reforestation or vegetation restoration, to mitigate landslide risks.
  • Identification of bare soil or exposed areas: NDVI can highlight areas with low or negative values, indicating the absence or minimal presence of vegetation cover. Bare soil or exposed areas are more susceptible to erosion and slope instability, increasing the risk of landslides. By mapping areas with low NDVI values, landslide susceptibility assessments can focus on areas requiring interventions to improve vegetation cover.
  • Monitoring vegetation changes: NDVI analysis allows for monitoring vegetation changes over time. Sudden declines or shifts in NDVI values may indicate vegetation stress, such as due to deforestation, land degradation, or disease, which can compromise slope stability. Monitoring vegetation changes through NDVI helps identify areas undergoing degradation and assess their potential impact on landslide susceptibility.
  • Integration with other data: NDVI data can be integrated with other factors such as topography, geology, and rainfall patterns to create comprehensive landslide susceptibility maps. By combining NDVI with these data layers, the influence of vegetation on slope stability can be assessed in conjunction with other contributing factors, enhancing the accuracy of the susceptibility mapping.

NDVI, as a valuable remote sensing tool, provides crucial information on vegetation health, density, and changes, enabling a better understanding of the role of vegetation in slope stability and landslide susceptibility. Integrating NDVI data into landslide susceptibility mapping allows for targeted interventions and management strategies to mitigate landslide risks.

Plan

Planimetric curves in landslide susceptibility mapping refer to the representation of slope contours or elevation lines on a 2D map, providing a visual depiction of the terrain's shape and identifying areas with potentially higher susceptibility to landslides.

Plan

Planimetric curves play a significant role in landslide susceptibility mapping by providing valuable information about the terrain's shape and influencing factors such as slope steepness, aspect, and drainage patterns, which are crucial in assessing landslide susceptibility. Here are some specific ways in which planimetric curves impact landslide susceptibility mapping:

  • Slope characterization: Planimetric curves depict slope contours, allowing for the characterization of slope steepness and gradient. Steeper slopes are generally more prone to landslides, and the visualization of slope contours helps identify areas with higher susceptibility to slope failures.
  • Aspect determination: By representing the terrain's shape and elevation lines, planimetric curves help determine the aspect or compass direction that a slope faces. This information is essential as different aspects receive varying amounts of sunlight and influence factors such as moisture content, vegetation distribution, and thermal conditions, all of which affect slope stability and landslide susceptibility.
  • Drainage analysis: Planimetric curves provide insights into the drainage patterns of an area. By examining the elevation lines and slope contours, information about natural watercourses, stream networks, and potential water accumulation areas can be derived. Proper drainage is crucial for slope stability, and understanding the drainage patterns aids in assessing landslide susceptibility.
  • Geomorphological features identification: Planimetric curves aid in identifying specific geomorphological features such as ridges, valleys, or concave/convex slopes. These features have distinct characteristics that can influence slope stability and landslide susceptibility. For example, concave slopes may accumulate water and sediments, increasing the potential for landslides.
  • Integration with other data: Planimetric curves can be integrated with other data layers such as geology, soil properties, land use, and precipitation patterns to create comprehensive landslide susceptibility maps. By combining planimetric curves with these data, a more accurate assessment of landslide susceptibility can be achieved by considering the terrain's shape in conjunction with other contributing factors.
  • Visualization and communication: The visual representation of the terrain through planimetric curves aids in communicating and interpreting the landscape's characteristics related to landslide susceptibility. It helps stakeholders, researchers, and decision-makers understand the spatial distribution of slopes and potential landslide-prone areas more intuitively.

Planimetric curves, by providing a visual depiction of slope contours and elevation lines, contribute to the understanding of terrain characteristics and their influence on landslide susceptibility. Integrating planimetric curves with other data layers enhances the accuracy of susceptibility mapping, aiding in effective landslide risk assessment and management.

Population

Population, in the context of landslide susceptibility mapping, refers to the presence and distribution of people in areas prone to landslides, highlighting the potential impact on human lives and infrastructure.

Population

Population has a significant impact on landslide susceptibility mapping in the following ways:

  • Exposure and vulnerability assessment: The presence of a dense population in landslide-prone areas increases the potential exposure and vulnerability to landslides. Mapping population distribution helps identify areas where human lives, settlements, and infrastructure are at higher risk, enabling targeted mitigation measures and evacuation plans.

Understanding the potential impact of landslides on infrastructure helps prioritize risk reduction efforts, infrastructure planning, and emergency response preparedness.
  • Land-use planning and development: Population data informs land-use planning decisions by identifying areas with higher population density that may require stricter regulations or zoning restrictions to prevent further development in landslide-prone zones. It assists in determining suitable areas for urban expansion, considering landslide susceptibility and potential risks to the population.
  • Risk communication and awareness: Incorporating population data into landslide susceptibility mapping helps in effectively communicating the risks associated with landslides to the affected communities. Understanding the population at risk allows for targeted awareness campaigns, education programs, and early warning systems to enhance preparedness and response capabilities.
  • Socio-economic considerations: Population data considers socio-economic factors, such as income levels, poverty rates, and social vulnerability, which influence the capacity of communities to cope with landslides. It helps identify vulnerable groups that may require additional support and resources during landslide events, ensuring an equitable approach to landslide risk reduction.
  • Data integration: Integrating population data with other geospatial data layers, such as topography, geology, rainfall patterns, and vegetation cover, improves the accuracy of landslide susceptibility mapping. The combination of population data with these factors provides a comprehensive understanding of the areas at risk and aids in effective decision-making and resource allocation.

Population data serves as a crucial component in landslide susceptibility mapping, helping to assess the potential impact on human lives, infrastructure, and socio-economic factors. It guides risk reduction strategies, land-use planning, and community engagement efforts, ultimately contributing to the overall resilience of landslide-prone areas.

Profile

Profile curves in landslide susceptibility mapping refer to the representation of elevation changes along a line or profile, providing insights into slope variations, height differences, and potential landslide-prone areas.

Profile

Profile curves have a significant impact on landslide susceptibility mapping in the following ways:

  • Slope characterization: Profile curves provide information about the variations in slope steepness and elevation along a specific line or profile. This helps in characterizing the terrain and identifying areas with steeper slopes, which are more prone to landslides.
  • Height differences: By analyzing profile curves, variations in elevation or height differences can be observed. Sudden changes in elevation may indicate potential geological discontinuities or differences in soil properties, which can influence slope stability and landslide susceptibility.
  • Identification of slope breaks: Profile curves help identify breaks or changes in slope profiles, such as convex or concave shapes. These breaks can indicate potential instability zones and areas where landslides are more likely to occur.
  • Cross-sectional analysis: Profile curves enable cross-sectional analysis of the terrain, providing insights into the geological and geomorphological features that contribute to landslide susceptibility. This analysis includes factors such as the presence of weak or unstable layers, fault lines, or changes in lithology, which play a crucial role in slope stability assessment.
  • Integration with other data: Profile curves can be integrated with other data layers, such as geological maps, land cover data, and rainfall patterns, to create comprehensive landslide susceptibility maps. By combining profile curve information with these data, a more accurate assessment of slope stability and landslide susceptibility can be achieved.
  • Visualization and communication: Profile curves provide a visual representation of the slope variations along a specific line, making it easier to communicate and interpret the terrain's characteristics related to landslide susceptibility. This visualization helps stakeholders, researchers, and decision-makers understand the slope profiles and potential landslide-prone areas more effectively.

By analyzing profile curves, landslide susceptibility mapping can gain valuable insights into slope characteristics, height differences, and potential instability zones. Integration with other data layers enhances the accuracy of susceptibility assessments, aiding in effective landslide risk assessment and management.

Rivers

Rivers, in the context of landslide susceptibility mapping, represent natural watercourses that can influence slope stability and contribute to the identification of areas prone to landslides.

Rivers

Rivers have a significant impact on landslide susceptibility mapping in the following ways:

  • Water saturation and erosion: Rivers can contribute to increased water saturation of slopes and erosion of soil and rock materials, which are key triggers for landslides. By mapping river networks and understanding their proximity to slopes, areas susceptible to landslides can be identified based on the potential influence of water on slope stability.
  • Channel migration and bank erosion: River channels that migrate or erode their banks can directly affect the stability of adjacent slopes. Mapping the historical patterns of channel migration and bank erosion helps identify areas where the proximity to the river poses a higher risk of landslides.
  • Sediment deposition: Rivers transport and deposit sediments downstream, which can accumulate at the base of slopes and increase the weight and instability of the slope material. By assessing the proximity of slopes to sediment deposition zones, areas with higher landslide susceptibility can be identified.
  • Hydraulic pressure: Rivers exert hydraulic pressure on slopes, especially during high flow events. This pressure can weaken the slope material and increase the likelihood of landslides. Mapping the relationship between river proximity, flow dynamics, and slope stability provides insights into areas where hydraulic pressure may pose a significant risk.
  • River-induced erosion and undercutting: Rivers can erode the base of slopes, leading to undercutting and loss of support for the slope material. This undercutting weakens the slope's stability and increases the susceptibility to landslides. Identifying areas where rivers have a history of undercutting slopes helps in identifying zones prone to slope failures.
  • Drainage patterns: River networks are indicators of the natural drainage patterns in a region. Mapping rivers and their tributaries assists in understanding the spatial distribution of water runoff and potential pathways for excess water during rainfall events. Areas with inadequate natural drainage can experience higher water infiltration, saturation, and increased landslide susceptibility.

By considering the influence of rivers on slope stability, landslide susceptibility mapping can better assess the areas prone to landslides and understand the mechanisms by which rivers contribute to slope failures. This information is vital for land-use planning, infrastructure development, and risk reduction strategies in landslide-prone regions.

Roads

Roads, in the context of landslide susceptibility mapping, represent infrastructure that can be affected by landslides and serve as indicators of potential instability in adjacent slopes.

Roads

Roads have a significant impact on landslide susceptibility mapping in the following ways:

  • Landslide occurrence and inventory: Roads are often affected by landslides, and mapping historical landslide occurrences along road networks helps identify areas prone to landslides. Road inventories provide valuable data on the spatial distribution and extent of landslides, aiding in the assessment of landslide susceptibility.
  • Slope stability assessment: Roads traverse slopes and can indicate areas with potential slope instability. Mapping roads helps identify sections that are susceptible to landslides based on factors such as slope steepness, geological conditions, and previous landslide incidents. This information contributes to slope stability assessments and prioritization of mitigation measures.
  • Indicators of slope movement: Roads can exhibit signs of slope movement, such as cracks, subsidence, or deformation. These indicators serve as valuable early warning signs of potential landslides. Mapping road conditions and monitoring changes over time can provide insights into the dynamic nature of slopes and their susceptibility to landslides.
  • Accessibility and infrastructure vulnerability: Road networks play a crucial role in connecting communities and facilitating transportation. Mapping roads helps assess the vulnerability of transportation infrastructure to landslides, identifying critical routes that may be at higher risk. This information assists in planning emergency response, evacuation routes, and the prioritization of infrastructure maintenance and protection measures.
  • Land-use planning and development: Roads influence land-use patterns, development, and urban expansion. Considering roads in landslide susceptibility mapping helps guide land-use planning decisions, such as avoiding construction in high-risk areas or implementing engineering measures to stabilise slopes along road alignments.
  • Data integration: Integrating road data with other geospatial information, such as topography, geology, rainfall patterns, and vegetation cover, enhances the accuracy of landslide susceptibility mapping. By combining road data with these factors, a more comprehensive understanding of the interplay between roads, slopes, and landslide susceptibility can be achieved.

Roads provide valuable insights into slope stability and serve as indicators of potential landslide-prone areas. Their mapping and analysis contribute to effective landslide susceptibility mapping, risk assessment, and infrastructure planning in landslide-prone regions.

Slope

Steep slopes increase the likelihood of landslides due to reduced stability and weaker resistance to gravitational forces.

Slope

Slope plays a fundamental role in landslide susceptibility mapping. Susceptibility mapping aims to identify areas that are more prone to landslides based on various contributing factors. Here's how slope impacts landslide susceptibility mapping:

  • Quantifying slope steepness: Slope steepness is one of the primary factors considered in landslide susceptibility mapping. It is usually quantified using slope angles or gradient measurements. Steeper slopes are generally associated with higher landslide susceptibility, as they have a weaker resistance to gravitational forces and are more prone to failure.
  • Classifying slope categories: Slopes are typically classified into different categories based on their steepness. These categories can range from gentle slopes (low gradient) to very steep slopes (high gradient). By assigning different weights or probabilities to each slope category, susceptibility maps can be created to highlight areas with varying degrees of landslide susceptibility.
  • Influence on other factors: Slope steepness can also influence other contributing factors that contribute to landslide susceptibility. For example, steep slopes tend to have poorer drainage, which can result in higher water content and increased pore pressures. This, in turn, weakens the slope materials and makes them more susceptible to landslides. Steeper slopes can also enhance the effects of erosion and sediment transport, further increasing landslide susceptibility.
  • Incorporating slope stability analysis: In landslide susceptibility mapping, slope stability analysis is often employed to assess the potential for slope failure. The stability analysis takes into account factors such as slope angle, soil properties, groundwater conditions, and triggering events to evaluate the stability of the slope. The results of such analyses can be used to identify areas with higher susceptibility to landslides.
  • Integration with other factors: Slope steepness is typically combined with other contributing factors, such as geology, soil type, land cover, precipitation patterns, and historical landslide data, to create comprehensive landslide susceptibility maps. By considering slope steepness along with these factors, a more accurate and reliable assessment of landslide susceptibility can be achieved.

It is important to note that landslide susceptibility mapping is a complex process that requires the integration of multiple factors and data sources. Slope steepness is just one aspect among many that contribute to the overall understanding of landslide susceptibility in a given area.

Test and Train

The landslide susceptibility assessment utilized 700 points for training and 199 points for testing the model's performance, ensuring robustness and evaluation of the model's accuracy.

Test and Train

In the landslide susceptibility assessment, the dataset comprised 700 points from two distinct groups: the landslide zone group and the non-landslide zone group, which were used for training the model. An additional 199 points, also from the two groups, were reserved for testing the model's performance. This approach ensured that the model was exposed to a diverse range of data representing both landslide-prone areas and stable/non-landslide areas. By training and testing the model on separate groups, it aimed to capture the distinguishing characteristics and patterns between these zones, enhancing its ability to accurately predict landslide susceptibility in similar areas with unknown outcomes. This methodology provided a rigorous evaluation of the model's effectiveness in distinguishing between landslide and non-landslide zones, thus improving the reliability and practical application of the landslide susceptibility assessment.
